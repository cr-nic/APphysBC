Electric Charges, Fields, and Gauss's Law

Introduction to E&M

The world around us is full of invisible forces which interact with us on a daily basis. Without them, we would be unable to have life as we know it, and having learned about them, we have been able to make technological leaps and bounds as a species. The three invisible forces which we interact with the most are electricity, magnetism, and gravity.

Now, gravity is something you should have already learned about, perhaps even on your first day of physics (remember? ?), and magnetism is something we'll discuss in the future. So electricity is what we'll talk about first. Great. What is it? Sure, it may power all your electronics, from the fridge in your home, to the diodes of the display on which you're reading this, but before we even get to how that works—to how electrons move—we need to establish what an electron even is.

Charge // What is an electron?

Everything you see and use on a regular basis is likely filled with free electrons, or electrons which are not strongly attracted to the nuclei of any atom. As the name implies, they are free to move around, being pushed and pulled every which way. As you use and touch things, their surfaces are likely getting covered in these free electrons. When an object has an abundnce of these free electrons, it has excess charge. For reasons you will see later, electrons do not like to be in regions of excess charge.

Here, you can see the surfaces of two objects on the atomic scale. The object on the top has an excess of free electrons, giving it a relative negative charge, while the object on the bottom has a scarcity of free electrons, giving it a relative positive charge. Assuming the two objects are great conductors, let's see what happens when we touch them together.

The two objects have now discharged—the free electrons between them have been equally distributed, and the excess charge no longer means anything for these two objects. You may have noticed such a discharge before when touching a doorknob, or using a woolen blanket, as a small "zap". That is exactly what is happening here. The positively charged region attracts the negative charge, and they each now have identical charges. In this case, they are now neutrally charged. This concept is not quite useful as of yet.

Now, let's turn the bottom object into a resistive material, and see what happens.

The molecules on the bottom have been changed into representations of their charges. The blue, being the negative charges of the electrons which can move about their nuclei (but as this is an insulator, cannot leave their valence shells), and the positive charges of their nuclei.

As we push the objects together, the excessively charged object's negative charges (being more plentiful, thanks to its addition of free electrons on top of those stuck close to their atoms), repel the insulator's negative charges. This is a demonstration of the Law of Charges: opposite charges attract, and negative charges repel. The insulator, however, does not have the structure for its electrons to become loose and for its charges to move freely, so they are only displaced by a small amount (this is also why the insulator doesn't discharge the charged object; there is nowhere for those free electrons to go). This effect, where charges are moved, but not entirely decoupled from the positive charges of their respective nuclei, is called polarization.

The most important part of this demonstration is the charged object's negative charges, and how they are attracted by the insulator's positive charges. The object's free electrons never leave it, but they still move, meaning that the object itself must also be pulled towards the insulator. Where there is movement of an object, there is acceleration, and where there is acceleration, there is force. This new force, is going to be our focus for the time being.

But first, let's use this with some physics we already know.

Example

Let's say you rub a balloon against your hair*, then put it up against a wall. It somehow sticks. Why?

  1. The balloon picks up free electrons from your hair.
  2. Electrons are much easier to move than protons.
  3. Protons pull electrons, creating a force.
  4. Net forces are theoretically equal to zero, therefore the balloon sticks to the wall

Considerations for signs of change

When two particles repel or attract each other, standard conventions for sign changes apply. This means that any movement to the left, or down, is positive, and any movement to the right, or up, is positive.
